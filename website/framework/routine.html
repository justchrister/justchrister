the framework / routine

3.1. Deep work

First thing in the morning, I create a distraction free environment to focus on a single problem or strategic decision. shutting down chat apps, moving my phone to another room, and having water, coffee, and snacks at the ready.

Even when the problem or decision seems obvious, I break it down from first principles. This approach helps me get to the core of the issue and discover innovative solutions.

The ability to think deep and focus on a single problem is a muscle, if you don't exercise it regularly, you will lose it.

3.3. Triaging

The most effective way to maintain a radical focus on execution is through triaging tasks. By consistently evaluating and categorizing tasks, I ensure that at any given moment, I know exactly what to focus on to drive the most impact.

Nothing low-impact: About 50% of the tasks I put into the list is set to dont meaning I will not prioritize it at all, it does not go into a “backlog of death”, I actually just kill it immediately.

3.4. Timeboxing

I review both my personal and work-related schedules to understand the meetings I need to attend. These meetings are then blocked out in a dedicated time management page in Notion. Once I have a clear picture of my scheduled commitments, I assess my priorities and their respective deadlines. This allows me to allocate time for deep work, shallow work, and personal tasks throughout the day. I use a time-boxing approach, which helps to structure my day while maintaining flexibility. The time-boxing schedule consists of three columns:

- timeslot: A specific block of time designated for a task.

- activity: The planned task or activity for that time slot.

- adjusted: A flexible option if priorities shift or something more important arises. This method ensures that while I plan my day in detail, I’m not locked into a rigid schedule. If a higher-priority task higher-priority task comes up, I adjust my plan without losing focus or momentum.

3.4. Shallow work

Shallow work consists of tasks that are logistical or operational in nature, such as project meetings, emails, Slack conversations, and administrative work. While these tasks are necessary to keep a team functioning smoothly, they don’t usually require intense focus or creative problem-solving. However, they require you to have thought through strategy during deep work sessions, enabling you to make crucial decisions that keep momentum going. The key is to keep shallow work structured and efficient, ensuring that it doesn’t creep into your deep work time. Set aside specific time blocks for responding to emails, participating in meetings, and managing operational.

3.5. Shutdown routine

After a long day of writing to-do’s, commiting to following up on things, setting up meeting points, you need to have a clear “end” to your work day. While having a clear “im done with work” point will not be possible during critical points in your startup journey or when managing large feature releases—where I sometimes work 14-hour days—having a shutdown routine is crucial both in normal times as well as during these crucial moments.

During the crucial moments I still do the shutdown routine between the 6-8 hour “shift”, and do something entirely different. This shutdown between “shifts” help me mentally reset, maintain productivity, and avoid burnout. It allows me to be fully present on whatever personal thing I am doing, whether that is going to an art exhibition, working out, or spending quality time with the people I love.

The main objective is preventing long-term burnout, the fact that I feel “on top of things” even when things are seemingly going to crash and burn, means that I dont take it with me to other fullfilling and important parts of my life. As I am present in those moments it is easier to get back to work with a positive attitude and outlook, making a high stress reality into a sustainable lifestyle. Of course this is also supported by the rest of this framework.
